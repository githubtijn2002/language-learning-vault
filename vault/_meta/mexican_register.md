## Diminutives — use moderately

Mexican Spanish leans on diminutives, though less aggressively than Andean varieties. Common ones:

- **ahorita** (very Mexican — vague: "in a bit" / "in an hour" / sometimes "right now")
- **tantito** (a little bit)
- **rapidito** (quickly)
- **poquito** (a little)
- **momentito** (one moment)
- **cerquita** (close by)
- **suavecito** (gently)

## Sentence patterns

- **"Es que…"** prefix to soften statements or excuses.
- **"Pues…"** opener, used heavily as a hedge particle.
- **"¿No?"** appended tag question (peninsular *¿verdad?* also fine, both common).
- **"Mira…"** for opening a friendly explanation.
- **"Híjole"** (mild exclamation: "wow / oof").

## Register hierarchy in Mexico (workplace-focused for Maya)

- ***usted*** = default with **leadership, clients, older colleagues, strangers in formal settings, all interactions in the first weeks at a new job**. Stay in *usted* until the other party switches first.
- ***tú*** = peers after onboarding, friends, anyone visibly younger, casual social settings.
- **Voseo** = not Mexican (Argentina, Uruguay, parts of Central America). Avoid.

Claude in this skill addresses Maya with ***tú*** by default (we're peers). When she's prepping for client / leadership dialogues, switch to *usted*.

## Avoid

- **vosotros** and all its conjugations (*habláis*, *tenéis*) — Spain only, not used in Mexico.
- Spain-isms: *vale, guay, tío/tía, molar, currar, joder, coger* (in Mexico *coger* = vulgar, use *tomar* / *agarrar*).
- Argentinianisms: *vos, che, boludo, pibe*.
- Colombianisms: *parce/parcero, chévere, vaina, dar papaya, ¿cómo así?*.
- Caribbean Spanish dropped /s/: *má'* for *más*, *etá* for *está*. Mexican Spanish keeps the /s/.

## Pronunciation notes (for shadow review)

- Mexican Spanish is **clear and consonant-faithful** — no aspirated /s/, full /d/ between vowels.
- *ll* and *y* merge into [ʝ] (yeísmo) — *calle* and *cayó* sound the same. Not *sh* (that's rioplatense), not *ll* (that's Andean).
- *s* always fully pronounced.
- Intonation is somewhat **sing-song** in CDMX — pitch rises and falls more than in flat Iberian Spanish.
- *x* in indigenous-origin words is unpredictable: México [ˈmexiko], Xochimilco [sotʃiˈmilko], Oaxaca [waˈxaka]. Learn case-by-case.
